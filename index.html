The Price of Life.
Five countries,
one currency.

A side-by-side reckoning of what it costs to live in Israel, Sweden, France, Russia and Slovakia — and what those countries pay you in return. Everything below is normalized to euros, weighted against median net income, and benchmarked against authoritative sources (OECD, Eurostat, INSEE, CBS, SCB, Rosstat, ŠÚ SR, Numbeo, The Economist). Slovakia is the new entrant: euro-using, EU-member, with the lowest income inequality in the entire EU and the seventh-heaviest tax wedge in the OECD — a useful mid-point against which the others reveal their extremes. § 00 · Method

How to read this.

Every number here is a model, not a fact. Salaries differ by region. Rents differ by contract. Tax effective rates depend on dependents. What follows is the central tendency for the central case — single adult or family of four in the capital — drawn from national statistical offices and OECD comparators.

Exchange rates & data vintage

All non-euro amounts converted using late-2024 / early-2025 reference rates: 1 ILS = 0.25 EUR (≈ 4.00 ILS / €), 1 SEK = 0.087 EUR (≈ 11.5 SEK / €), 1 RUB = 0.010 EUR (≈ 100 RUB / €). Slovakia and France use the euro natively. Period of measurement is the most recent available — 2024 for INSEE, SCB, Rosstat and ŠÚ SR salary structures, October 2024 for CBS Israel, December 2024 for Eurostat Gini, January 2025 for the Israeli VAT raise to 18%, the new Russian progressive tax scale, and the Slovak VAT raise to 23%. 2024 OECD Tax Wedge, January 2025 Big Mac Index, Numbeo 2025 quarterly indices.

Important methodology update (issue 03). Affordability ratios are now computed on a metro-area basis: capital-city rents and capital-city prices are compared against capital-city median net salaries, not national medians. This corrects an earlier bias in which Tel-Aviv/Paris/Stockholm/Moscow/Bratislava prices were paired against people who don't live there. Metro median net salaries used: Tel Aviv €2,800 · Stockholm €2,640 · Paris/IDF €2,730 · Moscow €1,044 · Bratislava €1,420. National medians remain in Chart 1 (income distribution) and Chart 3 (inequality) since those describe the country as a whole.

Russia-specific caveats. Russia was suspended from the OECD in March 2022, so cross-country OECD metrics (tax wedge, PPP wages) are unavailable for Russia in the 2024 round; the figures used here are from Rosstat, Bank of Russia, Moscow Times reporting, and Numbeo. The ruble has been highly volatile (RUB/EUR ranged 0.0085 → 0.0115 across 2024–25); a clean midpoint of 1 RUB = €0.010 is used throughout. Numbers from Rosstat are subject to ongoing methodology disputes (HSE 2025 critique noted in sources). Big Mac figures are imputed from the Vkusno i tochka "Big Hit" replacement, since McDonald's exited Russia in 2022.

Honest disclosure. The orchestrator pulled live figures via web search for the load-bearing numbers (INSEE, SMIC, Navigo, OECD wages, Israeli VAT, Numbeo, CBS, SCB, Stockholm/Tel Aviv/Moscow rents, Rosstat wages, Russian VAT and tax brackets). Some second-order numbers (utility bills, mid-tier salaries) are best-effort triangulations from multiple secondary sources. Sources are listed in full at the bottom of this document.

§ 01 · Income

What you make.

Median, mean, and the long tail. France and Sweden cluster around a similar median net wage; Israel sits lower at the median but spectacularly higher at the top of the tech tail; Russia sits well below all three on every measure — even Moscow tech, the elite Russian wage tier, falls short of an entry-level Swedish teacher.

Chart 2 · By profession · gross monthly EUR

A teacher, a nurse, a cashier, a bus driver — and an engineer.

Five representative occupations, gross monthly pay, all in euros. The Israeli tech bar is taller than every other bar on the chart by a wide margin — including a Swedish engineer at the same role.

The median Israeli earns less than the median Frenchman or Swede — but an Israeli software engineer in Tel Aviv earns roughly €2,600 more per month than her Stockholm counterpart, €3,100 more than her Parisian one, and about 3× what a Moscow software engineer takes home.

Chart 3b · Wealth (net per adult, €) · P10 → P90

Distribution by wealth, not income.

Net household wealth per adult (assets minus debt) — a different and much more unequal story than income. Wealth Ginis are universally higher than income Ginis because pension savings, property and equity holdings are concentrated at the top in ways that monthly wages aren't. Russia drops to a median of ~€10,000 (a generation of ruble devaluation + war + capital flight); Sweden's median is modest but its Gini is among the EU's highest — household wealth is concentrated despite egalitarian incomes. Slovakia inverts both: small median, but a wealth Gini below 0.40 thanks to widespread homeownership and low household debt.

§ 03 · Tax

What the state takes.

OECD Tax Wedge 2024 — total income tax plus employee and employer social contributions as a share of the total labour cost for a single worker at the average wage. Plus VAT, which everyone pays every day.

Chart 4 · Tax wedge & VAT

France runs one of the heaviest wedges in the OECD; Israel runs one of the lightest.

Tax wedge = (income tax + employee social security + employer social security) ÷ total labour cost. The OECD average for 2024 was 34.9%. VAT is the standard consumption rate. The four bars are different rate concepts plotted on the same axis for visual comparison — they should not be summed. * Russia was suspended from the OECD in 2022 — its tax wedge is imputed from employer social contributions (~30%) plus employee NDFL (13%). Top marginal rate of 22% applies only to annual income above ₽50M; the flat 13% still applies to ~95% of earners.

§ 04 · Basket

The monthly basket.

Seven categories that every adult pays. Each row shows the absolute price in euros, then the same price expressed as a share of median net monthly income. Israel's "expensive" comes mostly from groceries; Sweden's "expensive" reputation evaporates outside rent and food.

Chart 5 · Monthly basics · single adult, capital city · EUR

Where the money goes.

Average monthly cost in euros for the single-adult basket. Groceries, electricity, water, internet + mobile, public transport monthly pass, restaurant once a week, basic supplementary health.

Chart 6 · Each category as a % of national median net pay

Same basket, but weighed against what you make.

Indexed against metro median net pay (not national), the picture tightens dramatically. Israel's grocery bill is still 67% higher than France's in absolute euros (€500 vs €300), but on Tel Aviv's metro median net it consumes 18% — versus 11% in Paris and 9% in Stockholm. Russia's €140 grocery bill on Moscow's metro median (€1,044) is 13% — comparable to France. The earlier "Russia inverts everything" narrative was an artifact of pairing Moscow prices against the much lower national median; on a metro-vs-metro basis, Russia is mid-pack on food affordability.

§ 06 · Childcare

The cost of having a two-earner household.

Daycare for one toddler — the gateway expense that decides whether the second parent goes back to work. Sweden's maxtaxa cap is the single most aggressive family subsidy in the developed world; Israel's private maon is the most expensive.

Chart 9 · Monthly fee · one full-time toddler place · EUR

Daycare, after subsidy.

Sweden's förskola is capped at €155 per month regardless of household income (the famous maxtaxa; SEK 1,783 ceiling from Jan 2025, raised from SEK 1,688 / €147 in 2024). France's municipal crèche typically lands at €300–€500 after CAF subsidies; a private crèche or assistante maternelle nets to roughly €700 after the CMG, which covers up to 85% of expenses. In Israel, a private maon runs €950 unsubsidized, with means-tested public placement at €300–€475. Russia's public detsky sad is the cheapest in the comparison — by law municipalities may charge parents only up to 20% of total cost, so monthly fees in Moscow run around €24; private daycare starts at ~€250.

§ 08 · PPP

The Big Mac sanity check.

A Big Mac is a globally standardized basket of inputs — beef, bread, lettuce, packaging, real estate, labour, electricity, tax. The price tells you how a currency stacks up to a US dollar of purchasing power, no model required.

Chart 10 · Big Mac price · Jan 2025 · local price ↔ EUR

The hamburger doesn't lie.

Each Big Mac price (USD/€-converted, Jan 2025 Economist BMI) and the country's Numbeo Cost of Living Index (NYC = 100, excluding rent). Big Mac shows raw price level; Numbeo captures the whole basket. Both indicators put Tel Aviv at the top — Israel's Big Mac is roughly €6.10 (the most expensive of the five) and its Numbeo basket index sits at 76, against Bratislava's 56, Stockholm's 62 and Paris's 68. Russia, by contrast, posts the cheapest price level of the five in raw euros: Moscow's Numbeo CoLI sits at ~52 and the "Big Hit" (Vkusno i tochka's Big Mac replacement, since McDonald's exited in 2022) is under €2. The kicker is in the next chart: cheap prices ÷ low wages still produces the worst affordability ratios in the set.

§ 10 · The bottom line

So where is it cheapest to actually live?

There is no single answer — the five countries aren't optimized for the same thing. But for the median household, after the state is done taking and giving back, the ranking inverts the headlines. Russia is the most extreme case in either direction: brutal as a newcomer renting on the median wage, comfortable enough for the median household that inherited a Soviet-era flat.

1.
France
  • Real cost Heavy OECD tax wedge offset by a structurally cheap public sector: €178 university, €88.80 Navigo, free K-12, partially-reimbursed health. Paris/Île-de-France metro median net (€2,730) is the highest of the five.
  • Metro household surplus ≈ +€2,412/mo after the documented family basket.
  • Catch Mutuelle insurance is a hidden monthly cost that rises sharply with age; Paris-center rents harsh, banlieue moderate; the IDF premium drops outside the metro.
2.
Sweden
  • Real cost Highest tax wedge offset by capped childcare, free school + university, and a €122/yr healthcare ceiling. Cheapest effective bill per euro of metro income.
  • Metro household surplus ≈ +€2,357/mo after the documented family basket.
  • Catch Housing is bimodal — first-hand contracts require a 9–20 year queue; newcomers pay second-hand rates that close the gap with Paris.
3.
Israel
  • Real cost Lowest tax wedge of the five — but the lowest social return on it. Childcare, university, supplementary health all paid out of pocket. On a Tel Aviv-vs-Tel Aviv basis (not Tel Aviv-vs-Israel) the burden is comparable to Paris, not crushing.
  • Metro household surplus ≈ +€1,707/mo on Tel Aviv metro dual median (€2,800 net each).
  • Catch Tech-vs-rest stratification is the largest in the comparison: Tel Aviv tech is 2.8× the local median net, ~4× the national median net. Outside the metro the picture darkens sharply — the national-median Israeli runs a much tighter budget than the Tel Aviv-median one.
4.
Slovakia
  • Real cost 7th-heaviest OECD tax wedge (42.6%) but EU-priced, euro-denominated. Childcare cheap, university free, healthcare free, Big Mac €4.95. Bratislava regional median (€1,420 net) ~25% above national.
  • Metro household surplus ≈ +€458/mo on Bratislava regional dual median.
  • Catch Lowest Gini in the EU (0.217) means redistribution does the work — but regional inequality is among Europe's widest. Rural Slovakia is much closer to the bottom of the wage band; the metro premium is real.
5.
Russia
  • Real cost Cheapest prices in absolute euros — but lowest metro median income (€1,044 in Moscow). 13% flat income tax (22% from Jan 2025 above ₽50M/yr) and 20% VAT.
  • Metro household surplus ≈ +€399/mo on Moscow metro dual median (€2,088). Adding the ~80% home-ownership rate effectively pushes this to ≈+€1,600 for inherited-flat households.
  • Catch Sanctions-era data is contested. Rosstat wage growth (+19% nominal 2024) reflects a real war-economy labour shortage but also high inflation (8.5%). FX volatility can move every euro number here ±15% in a quarter.
Final note on weighting

The ranking above weights family-of-four affordability with dual metro median earners, paying rent in the capital. It would change if the persona were a single tech professional (Israel jumps to #1 by a wide margin), a retiree drawing pension (France's lower tax on pensions matters), a student (Sweden's €0 tuition is decisive), a Russian homeowner who inherited a Moscow flat (Russia climbs several places), or a non-metro Israeli teacher (Israel falls). The headline is that on a like-for-like metro basis the five cluster much closer than the old national-median framing suggested — but the within-country regional gap (especially Bratislava vs rural Slovakia, Tel Aviv vs Beersheba, Moscow vs anywhere else in Russia) is itself part of the answer to "how expensive is this country."
