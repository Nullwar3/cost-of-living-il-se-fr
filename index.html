Cost of living · Income · Inequality

The Price of Life.
Four countries,
one currency.

A side-by-side reckoning of what it costs to live in Israel, Sweden, France and Russia — and what those countries pay you in return. Everything below is normalized to euros, weighted against median net income, and benchmarked against authoritative sources (OECD, Eurostat, INSEE, CBS, SCB, Rosstat, Numbeo, The Economist). The answers are sharper than the conventional wisdom — and they hinge less on raw price levels than on what the state quietly takes off your plate. Russia enters the comparison as a cautionary fourth: very low absolute prices in euros, but median wages so low that the burden ratios are the most extreme in the set.

Israel · Tel Aviv

Two countries in one currency.

A tech worker in Tel Aviv earns 3.5× the national median gross wage (and over 4× the median net); a teacher earns less than the median rent. Housing alone — a 1BR in central Tel Aviv at €1,900 — consumes ≈103% of the median take-home pay.

High taxes hide a low real bill.

France · Paris

Highest PPP wages of the four, one of the heaviest tax wedges in the OECD (second only to Belgium), and a structurally cheap public sector — €178/year for a university degree, €88.80 for the Navigo monthly pass.

Russia · Moscow

Cheap in euros, brutal in rubles.

§ 00 · Method

How to read this.

Every number here is a model, not a fact. Salaries differ by region. Rents differ by contract. Tax effective rates depend on dependents. What follows is the central tendency for the central case — single adult or family of four in the capital — drawn from national statistical offices and OECD comparators.

Exchange rates & data vintage

All non-euro amounts converted using late-2024 / early-2025 reference rates: 1 ILS = 0.25 EUR (≈ 4.00 ILS / €), 1 SEK = 0.087 EUR (≈ 11.5 SEK / €), 1 RUB = 0.010 EUR (≈ 100 RUB / €). Period of measurement is the most recent available — 2024 for INSEE, SCB and Rosstat salary structures, October 2024 for CBS Israel, December 2024 for Eurostat Gini, January 2025 for the Israeli VAT raise to 18% and the new Russian progressive tax scale, 2024 OECD Tax Wedge, January 2025 Big Mac Index, Numbeo 2025 quarterly indices.

Russia-specific caveats. Russia was suspended from the OECD in March 2022, so cross-country OECD metrics (tax wedge, PPP wages) are unavailable for Russia in the 2024 round; the figures used here are from Rosstat, Bank of Russia, Moscow Times reporting, and Numbeo. The ruble has been highly volatile (RUB/EUR ranged 0.0085 → 0.0115 across 2024–25); a clean midpoint of 1 RUB = €0.010 is used throughout. Numbers from Rosstat are subject to ongoing methodology disputes (HSE 2025 critique noted in sources). Big Mac figures are imputed from the Vkusno i tochka "Big Hit" replacement, since McDonald's exited Russia in 2022.

Honest disclosure. The orchestrator pulled live figures via web search for the load-bearing numbers (INSEE, SMIC, Navigo, OECD wages, Israeli VAT, Numbeo, CBS, SCB, Stockholm/Tel Aviv/Moscow rents, Rosstat wages, Russian VAT and tax brackets). Some second-order numbers (utility bills, mid-tier salaries) are best-effort triangulations from multiple secondary sources. Sources are listed in full at the bottom of this document.

§ 01 · Income

What you make.

Median, mean, and the long tail. France and Sweden cluster around a similar median net wage; Israel sits lower at the median but spectacularly higher at the top of the tech tail; Russia sits well below all three on every measure — even Moscow tech, the elite Russian wage tier, falls short of an entry-level Swedish teacher.

§ 03 · Tax

What the state takes.

OECD Tax Wedge 2024 — total income tax plus employee and employer social contributions as a share of the total labour cost for a single worker at the average wage. Plus VAT, which everyone pays every day.

Chart 4 · Tax wedge & VAT

France runs one of the heaviest wedges in the OECD; Israel runs one of the lightest.

Tax wedge = (income tax + employee social security + employer social security) ÷ total labour cost. The OECD average for 2024 was 34.9%. VAT is the standard consumption rate. The four bars are different rate concepts plotted on the same axis for visual comparison — they should not be summed. * Russia was suspended from the OECD in 2022 — its tax wedge is imputed from employer social contributions (~30%) plus employee NDFL (13%). Top marginal rate of 22% applies only to annual income above ₽50M; the flat 13% still applies to ~95% of earners.

The monthly basket.

Seven categories that every adult pays. Each row shows the absolute price in euros, then the same price expressed as a share of median net monthly income. Israel's "expensive" comes mostly from groceries; Sweden's "expensive" reputation evaporates outside rent and food.

Chart 5 · Monthly basics · single adult, capital city · EUR

Where the money goes.

Average monthly cost in euros for the single-adult basket. Groceries, electricity, water, internet + mobile, public transport monthly pass, restaurant once a week, basic supplementary health.

Chart 6 · Each category as a % of national median net pay

Same basket, but weighed against what you make.

Israel's grocery bill is already 67% higher than France's in absolute euros (€500 vs €300). Indexed against median net pay it becomes punishing — 27% of take-home in Israel against 14% in France and 10% in Sweden. Russia inverts this entirely: groceries cost just €140 in absolute terms (a third of Israel's bill), but on the €491 median net wage that still consumes 29% — more than Israel. Cheap in dollars, expensive in your own currency.

Chart 8 · Home price ÷ annual gross salary

Buying, not renting.

How many years of gross salary at the national median does it take to buy a 4-room apartment in the capital? This is the price-to-income ratio Numbeo and the Bank for International Settlements use to flag housing-bubble territory. Tel Aviv sits in the global worst quintile; Moscow's 47-year ratio is technically the worst — but in practice softened by mass post-Soviet privatization, which means most current Russian residents didn't buy at market price.

An Israeli median earner would need 17 years of full gross pay to buy an average four-room apartment in Tel Aviv. In Stockholm: 11 years. In Paris: 14. In Moscow: 47 — though most Russians never had to pay it.

§ 06 · Childcare

The cost of having a two-earner household.

Daycare for one toddler — the gateway expense that decides whether the second parent goes back to work. Sweden's maxtaxa cap is the single most aggressive family subsidy in the developed world; Israel's private maon is the most expensive.

Chart 9 · Monthly fee · one full-time toddler place · EUR

Daycare, after subsidy.

Sweden's förskola is capped at €155 per month regardless of household income (the famous maxtaxa; SEK 1,783 ceiling from Jan 2025, raised from SEK 1,688 / €147 in 2024). France's municipal crèche typically lands at €300–€500 after CAF subsidies; a private crèche or assistante maternelle nets to roughly €700 after the CMG, which covers up to 85% of expenses. In Israel, a private maon runs €950 unsubsidized, with means-tested public placement at €300–€475. Russia's public detsky sad is the cheapest in the comparison — by law municipalities may charge parents only up to 20% of total cost, so monthly fees in Moscow run around €24; private daycare starts at ~€250.

§ 08 · PPP

The Big Mac sanity check.

A Big Mac is a globally standardized basket of inputs — beef, bread, lettuce, packaging, real estate, labour, electricity, tax. The price tells you how a currency stacks up to a US dollar of purchasing power, no model required.

Chart 10 · Big Mac price · Jan 2025 · local price ↔ EUR

The hamburger doesn't lie.

Each Big Mac price (USD/€-converted, Jan 2025 Economist BMI) and the country's Numbeo Cost of Living Index (NYC = 100, excluding rent). Big Mac shows raw price level; Numbeo captures the whole basket. Both indicators put Tel Aviv at the top — Israel's Big Mac is roughly €6.10 (the most expensive of the four) and its Numbeo basket index sits at 76, against Stockholm's 62 and Paris's 68. Russia, by contrast, posts the cheapest price level of the four in raw euros: Moscow's Numbeo CoLI sits at ~52 and the "Big Hit" (Vkusno i tochka's Big Mac replacement, since McDonald's exited in 2022) is under €2. The kicker is in the next chart: cheap prices ÷ low wages still produces the worst affordability ratios in the set.

§ 10 · The bottom line

So where is it cheapest to actually live?

There is no single answer — the four countries aren't optimized for the same thing. But for the median household, after the state is done taking and giving back, the ranking inverts the headlines. Russia is the most extreme case in either direction: brutal as a newcomer renting on the median wage, comfortable enough for the median household that inherited a Soviet-era flat.

1.
Sweden
  • Real cost Highest tax wedge is offset by capped childcare, free school + university, and a €122/yr healthcare ceiling. Effective bill for a family is the lowest of the four (on the renter-paying model).
  • Median household surplus ≈ €2,037/mo after the documented family basket.
  • Catch Housing is bimodal — first-hand contracts require a 9–20 year queue; newcomers pay second-hand rates that close the gap with Paris.
2.
France
  • Real cost Heaviest tax wedge in the OECD — but the public sector is structurally inexpensive: €178 university, €88.80 Navigo, free K-12, partially-reimbursed health.
  • Median household surplus ≈ €1,332/mo after the documented family basket.
  • Catch Mutuelle insurance is a hidden monthly cost that rises sharply with age; banlieue rent burden is moderate but Paris-center is harsh.
3.
Israel
  • Real cost Lowest tax wedge of the four (excluding Russia's flat-tax era) — but the lowest social return on it. Childcare, university, supplementary health all paid out of pocket; food and housing eat the rest.
  • Median household surplus ≈ −€193/mo — a small deficit on the central case. Reasonable variation brings it to roughly zero; cheaper rent further from the metro can push it positive.
  • Catch If you're in tech, none of this applies. The Israeli high-tech salary is the single most lucrative position in this comparison — but only ~10% of the workforce holds it.
4.
Russia
  • Real cost Prices the cheapest of the four in absolute euros — but wages so low that burden ratios are the worst in the set. 13% flat income tax (22% from Jan 2025 above ₽50M/yr) and 20% VAT.
  • Median household surplus ≈ −€707/mo on the renter model; ≈ +€500/mo for the ~80% of households who own outright. The "true" Russian budget depends almost entirely on which side of the housing inheritance line you fall.
  • Catch Sanctions-era data is contested. Rosstat's wage growth figures (+19% nominal in 2024) reflect a real war-economy labour shortage but also high inflation (8.5%). Foreign-currency conversion is volatile; ruble depreciation can change every figure here by ±15% within a quarter.
Final note on weighting

The "ranking" above weights family-of-four affordability with dual median earners in the capital city, paying rent. It would change if the persona were a single tech professional (Israel jumps to #1), a retiree drawing pension (France's lower tax on pensions matters), a student (Sweden's €0 tuition is decisive), or a Russian homeowner who inherited a Moscow flat (Russia jumps several places). The point isn't that one country is cheap and another expensive — it's that cost of living is a function of what the state already bought for you, what your parents owned before you, and which currency you happen to earn in.
